DATA SUMMARY

calls.day <- calls %>%
  group_by(date) %>%
  summarise(count = n())

first <- min(calls.day$date)
last <- max(calls.day$date)

calls.day <- calls.day %>% 
  complete(date = seq(ymd(first), 
                      ymd(last), 
                      "day")) %>%
  mutate(dow = wday(date, label = T, week_start = 1), 
         month.name = month(date, label = T, abbr = F),
         month.num = substr(date, 6,7),
         week = isoweek(date),
         day = day(date),
         year = year(date))

calls.day$count[is.na(calls.day$count)] = "0"
calls.day$count <- as.numeric(calls.day$count)

high <- as.numeric(max(calls.day$count))
low <- as.numeric(min(calls.day$count))
mean <- mean(calls.day$count)
sd <- sd(calls.day$count)

calls.day <- mutate(calls.day, 
                    week = case_when(month.name == "December" & week == 1 ~ 53,
                                     month.name == "January" & week %in% 52:53 ~ 0,
                                     TRUE ~ week),
                    category = cut(count, c(low-1, (mean-sd*3), (mean-sd*2), (mean-sd), mean, (mean+sd), (mean+sd*2), (mean+sd*3), high+1))) 

calls.summary <- calls.day %>%
  group_by(category) %>%
  summarise(count = n())

ANALYSIS

DAY OF WEEK The hotspots typically started on Fridays, moving to Saturdays, and then again on Tuesdays. The cold spots for days of the week usually took place Wednesday, Friday, and Sunday. This call subset progresses across time. This could be in part to the availability of officers, especially if there is a limited number of resources for this sort of checks due to the police department being run on a rotation schedule so this may force the department to conduct these types of checks on a specific day when officers who have been trained for this sort of call is available. For the upcoming week, I expect this same pattern to occur with a slight increase in the number of calls.

HOUR OF DAY:

The hotspots for the hour of day typically occur between the hours of 0900 2000. On Fridays the hours would be 1500 2000, Saturdays were 1300-1400, and on Tuesdays at 1700 hours. The cold spots usually occur during the 2300 0800 hours with a concentration on the same exact days. This could be due to the safety of the officers as well as the individuals because sunlight offers better visibility, and conducting these types of searches at certain times can allow time-sensitive needs or concerns to be addressed during business hours. Again, with the availability of trained personnel, these calls fall into a pattern of being conducted during regular business hours and not during shift changes.

MONTH OF YEAR:

The hotspots for month of year occurred between July and September, and also in the wintertime (January through May). The cold spots were sporadic, but they typically occurred in April and June. This call subset was low from 2007 until 2014, and that could be partly due to: mental health awareness, the call to focus on community policing, and laws or legislation mandating or encouraging welfare checks to enhance public safety as well as address societal concerns such as elderly abuse and child welfare issues. During the month of August, I expect to see the same pattern as before because August is the end of the summer but the beginning of the new school year when anxiety, depression, and other illnesses seem to peak other than during the holidays.
